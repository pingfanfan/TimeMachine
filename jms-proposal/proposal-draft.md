## 1. The Topic and Relationship of Interest

Generative AI — and large language models (LLMs) in particular — is rapidly entering the qualitative researcher's workflow. Scholars are already using GPT-class systems to generate codes from interview transcripts, surface themes from policy documents, summarize sprawling field-note corpora, and even propose theoretical frames (Christou, 2024; Hannigan et al., 2024). What once took months of close reading is, in early reports, completed in days. Yet the *theoretical* consequences of this shift have remained largely unexamined.

This proposal addresses a specific gap at the heart of this Special Issue's call: **how does the introduction of LLMs as analytical instruments change the kind of theory that qualitative management research can produce, and what method–theory framework is required to harness that shift responsibly?** We argue that the dominant framing — LLMs as faster, cheaper, more replicable coders — is methodologically thin and theoretically corrosive. It reduces theorizing to a downstream product of categorization, and risks producing an intellectual monoculture in which all inductive studies converge on the patterns most legible to a small number of foundation models.

We propose a different framing. We treat the human–LLM relationship in qualitative analysis as a *configurable theorizing regime* with three distinct modes — **augmentation**, **delegation**, and **automation** — each producing a different epistemic posture and, consequently, a different surface of theoretical possibilities. The choice of regime is not a methodological detail. It is a choice about what counts as a researcher, what counts as evidence, and what kinds of theory the field will see in the next decade.

Our work targets the long-standing JMS conversation on theoretical progress through methodological reflexivity (Alvesson & Sandberg, 2013; Cornelissen, 2017; Cornelissen & Durand, 2014; Harley & Faems, 2017; Lê & Schmid, 2022; Spicer & Alvesson, 2025). We extend this conversation by asking what theoretical pluralism requires when the analytical instrument itself begins to do interpretive work.

## 2. The Methodological Contribution

We make three connected methodological contributions.

### 2.1 A Three-Regime Framework

We propose a typology that distinguishes three modes of LLM involvement in inductive theorizing:

- **Augmentation.** The researcher remains the primary interpretive agent. The LLM is invoked at specific, theory-relevant junctures — e.g., to surface negative cases, propose counter-codes, identify latent contradictions across a corpus too large for one analyst, or stress-test an emerging frame against alternative interpretive vocabularies. The output is *provocations*, not codings; the human decides which provocations to pursue.

- **Delegation.** The researcher delegates a bounded analytical task — first-cycle in-vivo coding, sentiment classification, or topic modeling on a fixed taxonomy — and uses the LLM's output as raw material for second-cycle interpretive work. The boundary is contractual: the human retains theoretical authority but yields procedural authority.

- **Automation.** The LLM produces an end-to-end inductive analysis: codes, themes, and a draft theoretical model. The human role becomes auditing, not building. This is currently the most-commercialized mode (Atlas.ti's intelligent coder, NVivo's AI assistant, off-the-shelf wrappers around GPT-4) and, we will argue, the most theoretically dangerous when adopted uncritically.

Each regime has different consequences for **(i) the kinds of patterns that can surface** (regime determines visibility), **(ii) the locus of theoretical agency** (who is doing the imagining?), and **(iii) the auditable trail of interpretation** (what can a reader reconstruct?). Crucially, regime choice should be a *theoretical* decision, not a logistical one — and our framework makes this choice articulable and defensible.

### 2.2 An Epistemological Audit Protocol

To make the regime choice operational, we propose a four-step audit that authors can run on their own LLM-augmented work, and that reviewers can use to evaluate it:

1. **Input transparency.** Which prompts, system instructions, and model versions were used, with what temperature settings? Were transcripts modified before ingestion?
2. **Provocation logging.** When the LLM produced a code, theme, or interpretive suggestion, what did the researcher do with it? Adopt? Reject? Modify? Theorize against?
3. **Counterfactual probing.** Were alternative prompts run? Did changing the prompt frame change the surfaced patterns? If so, what does that variability mean for the resulting theory?
4. **Authorial accountability.** What in the final theoretical contribution can the researcher defend independently of the model output? What is irreducibly model-derived?

This protocol does not replace established standards for trustworthiness in qualitative work (Lincoln & Guba, 1985; Pratt et al., 2020). It supplements them with the specific accountability problems LLMs introduce — most obviously, the loss of interpretive provenance when generation and analysis collapse into the same artifact.

The demonstration is not designed to identify the "best" regime. It is designed to make visible what each regime makes visible, and what each makes invisible, so that future researchers can choose a regime *for theoretical reasons*.

## 3. The Novel Theoretical Implication

The novel theoretical contribution is twofold and operates at two levels.

### 3.1 A Theory of Theorizing-Regime Effects

At the **methodological-theory level**, we argue that the introduction of LLMs into qualitative analysis is not method-neutral. Different regimes systematically advantage different kinds of theory:

- *Augmentation* preserves the human's capacity for interpretive surprise — the moment when a researcher recognizes a phenomenon for which the existing theoretical vocabulary is inadequate (Locke et al., 2008). It is the regime most likely to yield genuinely novel theoretical *concepts* — but it is also slow, expensive, and requires substantial human expertise.
- *Delegation* is well-suited to scaling existing theoretical conversations to larger corpora. It is the regime most likely to advance *cumulative* theory — confirming, extending, and contextualizing established constructs across broader empirical bases.
- *Automation* tends toward theoretical *normalization*. Because foundation models encode the dominant theoretical vocabularies of their training data, end-to-end automated analyses are biased toward producing theories that resemble what the field has already produced. This is not a flaw of any specific model; it is an emergent property of treating an instrument trained on yesterday's literature as a producer of tomorrow's theory.

This claim has direct implications for the JMS debate on theoretical pluralism (Alvesson & Sandberg, 2013; Cornelissen, 2017). If the field drifts uncritically toward automation regimes — driven by speed, cost, and the apparent reproducibility of LLM outputs — we predict a structural compression of theoretical diversity. Methods, in other words, can *capture* a field (in the call's own language) by silently homogenizing what new theory is permitted to look like.

### 3.2 A Theory of GenAI as a Co-Theorizing Actor

At the **substantive-theory level**, our framework opens a new front in the debate on agency and the role of non-human actors in organizational life (Hannigan et al., 2024; Lebovitz et al., 2022). When an LLM contributes to inductive coding, theorizing, or model construction, the locus of agency in the research process is no longer the researcher alone. Yet the field's current frames for agency — derived from actor-network theory, sociomateriality, and practice theory — were not built with a generative, language-fluent, reflexive non-human actor in mind. We propose that LLM-augmented qualitative research is itself a *case site* for theorizing GenAI agency, and that our three-regime framework can travel from a methodological statement into a substantive one: organizations using GenAI in production work face structurally analogous questions about augmentation, delegation, and automation.

This double move — making a methodological framework do double duty as a substantive theoretical contribution about GenAI in organizing — is the kind of method–theory synergy the Special Issue explicitly invites (call language: *"substantive methodological synergies, whereby new methods provide novel insights into important substantive issues"*). It also speaks directly to the substantive priority of *managing the centrality of technology (e.g., GenAI as an actor; recasting the role of agency)* listed in the call.

## 4. Fit With the Special Issue Aims

We map our contribution to the call's five characteristics of successful submissions:

1. **Originality in methods and theorizing from such methods.** The three-regime framework is, to our knowledge, the first systematic articulation of LLM use in qualitative theorizing that treats regime choice as a theoretical (not procedural) decision.

2. **Originality in combinations of methods and theory.** Our empirical demonstration combines three analytical pipelines on a single corpus, with cross-regime comparison built in by design — an unusual combination that exists because the framework itself demands it.

3. **Promising new tools and technologies.** LLMs are explicitly named in the call as a qualitative-space technology of interest. We push beyond surface use toward a principled framework for incorporation.

4. **Reinvigorating theoretical bodies.** Inductive theorizing in management has been criticized for templating (Pratt et al., 2020) and for declining theoretical novelty over time. We argue that the augmentation regime, used deliberately, can interrupt templating by surfacing patterns the human eye misses — while we warn against the automation regime, which may accelerate templating's worst tendencies.

5. **Addressing current methodological challenges.** Our audit protocol directly addresses transparency, replication, and the validity of inferences drawn from generative-model output — three challenges named in the call.

## 6. Why JMS, Why This SI

JMS has been the principal venue for articles that ask the field to rethink its theorizing practices (Alvesson & Sandberg, 2013; Cornelissen, 2017; Cornelissen & Durand, 2014; Harley & Faems, 2017; Spicer & Alvesson, 2025; Wickert et al., 2021). The proposed paper continues this lineage and brings it forward into a moment in which the analytical instrument itself has begun to theorize. Other journals would treat this as a methods paper. JMS readership — and this Special Issue in particular — is the venue most likely to read it as what it is: a contribution about what management theory can become when its tools fundamentally change.
